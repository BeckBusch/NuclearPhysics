Power Plant Safety

What are Nuclear Power Plants?

Nuclear power plants harness the energy that is released when an atom splits through nuclear fission. Nuclear fission occurs when a neutron is absorbed into the nucleus of an atom, usually uranium-235, causing the atom to split into two smaller atoms while releasing three neutrons, several gamma rays, and a large amount of energy. This energy is used to heat up large volumes of water, which spin turbines that generate electricity. The three neurons that are released are able to continue on and cause more fission reactions. This creates a chain reaction which creates lots of energy very fast.

What are Control Rods?

Control rods are a safety feature in power plants that allows workers to slow down or stop the reaction by absorbing the neutrons without continuing the chain reaction. Materials that are not fissile are introduced into the reactor which absorb the neutrons. Because they are not fissile, the collision with the neutron does not cause another fission reaction, and those neutrons do not contribute to the chain reaction. The materials that these rods are made out of are chosen based on their ability to absorb neutrons, which is quantified using the neutron capture cross-section. This is the cross area of an atom that can absorb neutrons. A common choice for control rods is boron. Boron has a large neutron capture cross section, but because of the way that it's elementary form behaves, it is used in alloys, such as high boron steel.[1]

There is one important trait of nuclear fission that allows control rods to be effective. If neutrons could react with atoms adjacent to the atom that released them, then the control rods would somehow have to be placed in between the individual atoms of fuel. Luckily, this is not the case as neutrons cannot react as soon as they are released. When Nuclear fission releases neutrons, they have a lot of energy, and are moving at very fast speeds. However the likelihood of a reaction occuring depends on the amount of energy the neutron has, the less energy it has the higher the chance it will trigger a fission reaction. This means that until the neutrons have expended some of their energy by travelling away from the fuel rod that they came from, they will not react. This makes them travel through the space inbetween the fuel rods and allows them to be intercepted by a control rod.[2]


How do Containment Systems Work?

Between the gamma radiation, radioactive fuel, and high pressure steam, nuclear reactors have a lot of dangers that they need to protect against. The ideology for protecting against exposure or leaks is ‘Defence in Depth’, containing the nuclear reaction with four different systems.[3]
The first is the ceramic fuel pellets. Instead of using elemental uranium in the reactor, uranium dioxide is used, as its physical properties are favourable. Not only is its melting point much higher (2865°C vs 1132°C), because its already an oxide, it can’t burn. This drastically reduces complications. To create these pellets, uranium trioxide is reacted with hydrogen to form uranium oxide. The uranium oxide is then pressed into pellets, and sintered to form a ceramic.[4] Sintering is a process where the powder is heated up to near-melting. This causes it to join together and form a solid without needing to melt.
The second system is the fuel rods themselves. The fuel pellets are stacked and then sealed in tubes, along with coolant. These tubes are made of zirconium alloys, which are very corrosion resistant and have a low neutron capture area. The corrosion resistance prevents the fuel from leaking, while the low neutron capture area stops the neutrons interacting with something that’s not designed to control or limit the reaction.[5]
The final containment system is the containment building. When people think of a nuclear power plant, they imagine a large hyperbolic structure. This structure does not contain the reactor, but rather, is just a water cooling tower. The reactor is actually contained within a cylindrical building, built to contain high pressure steam, or radioactive gas in the case of an emergency. Although some are made with lead, the most common designs use concrete reinforced with steel.[3] This is because the containment building doesn’t need lead to block radiation, but rather high strength steel to contain steam pressure.

What Actually Contains the Reaction Itself?

The third layer of defence is the Reactor Pressure Vessel. This is where the reaction itself occurs, so the vessel needs to be able to withstand one of the most extreme environments sustained on earth. To resist corrosion, the parts of the vessel that are in contact with the coolant are made from stainless steel with a high chromium content. The main body of the vessel is constructed from plate steel with a high nickel content. Nickel and chromium are important in the vessel because they have a high heat and pressure resistance, making them resistant to shock. h
Corrosion is a big problem in the pressure vessel because the metal is constantly being bombarded with high energy particles. When these particles hit atoms in the steel, they can transfer their energy and knock the atom out of the lattis, creating defects in the metal which can turn into cracks, which isn’t the best thing to have in a pressure vessel.[23]

How does Water Cooling Work?

The majority of the worlds nuclear power plants are light water reactors. The phrase ‘light water’ means that the plant uses normal water instead of heavy water, which is water with hydrogen atoms that have extra neutrons. There are three types of light water reactors; pressurized water reactors, boiling water reactors, and supercritical water reactors, the latter being far less common than the first two.[6]
Any atoms in close proximity to radiation have the potential to absorb neutrons into their nucleus. When this happens, the atom can become unstable and emit these extra neutrons into the environment at predictable but ultimately random intervals. The large amount of water in a nuclear power plant are highly susceptible to absorbing neutrons and therefore large quantities of oxygen turn into nitrogen-16, a radioactive isotope that decays after 7 seconds to release a deadly gamma ray.[7]
Nitrogen-16 is responsible for one of the main flaws in boiling water reactors. Boiling waters pump water into the reactor core to the turned into steam. This steam then exits the reactor core and is taken through pipes to turbines, which are turned to generate electricity. The problem is, the highly radioactive nitrogen-16 has to be transported around the entire system. This means that almost all the pipes and turbine parts need to be resistant to radiation. The answer to this problem is pressurised water reactors. Pressurized water reactors use two water systems to contain the water that gets exposed to radiation. Just like BWRs, water is pumped into the reactor core to be heated. The difference is that this heated water is then used to heat more water while remaining in its pipes. This way the reactor can boil water while not creating large amounts of radioactive water.[6]

Power Plant Disaster

What is a Meltdown?

A nuclear meltdown is a severe accident that results from the core overheating and, as the name suggests, melting. If the nuclear fuel gets to the point where the cooling system can't cool it fast enough it will continue heating until it destroys the zirconium casing of the fuel rod and leaks out. The zirconium metal can, at such high temperatures, react with the uranium dioxide used as fuel to form metallic uranium.
  Zr + UO2 → ZrO2 + U [8]
Among other things, this lowers the melting point to the fuel, allowing it to leak out of the fuel rods and into the reactor. The now molten fuel will then continue to melt through most layers of the reactor, eventually interacting with the liquid coolant. This is very dangerous as the steam created can leak contaminants into the environment. Eventually the molten fuel will become corium; a lava-like mixture of many things in the reactor such as fuel, fission products, different metals, and even concrete. This mixture creates an eutectic system, meaning that melting point of the mixture is lower than the melting point of many of the individual substances. Because of this, it will stay liquid for longer and pose a much greater risk to leaking radioactive substances into the surrounding environment.[9]

What is a Steam Explosion?

A steam explosion is when water is boiled into steam in such extreme conditions, that the speed of the phase change (boiling from liquid to gas) creates an explosion similar to a combustion explosion. Although the heat and fire are damaging in a combustion explosion, the majority of the structural damage is caused by the rapid expansion of the gas or liquid. This rapid expansion is just as present in a steam explosion. The extreme pressure created by such an explosion can damage containment units and pressure chambers. This causes large cracks in structures, allowing radioactive material to escape. Another danger is the steam itself. Hydrogen can form many radioactive isotopes, and most of them are able to still form water. An isotope is an atom that has extra neutrons in its nucleus. Because these extra neutrons make the atom unstable, it will periodically release them into the environment as radiation. The water in a power plant has high exposure to the radioactive core and radioactive hydrogen is very common. If water made from radioactive hydrogen escapes from the reactor and floats away as steam, it can have a large impact on the surrounding environment.[10]

How does a Hydrogen Explosion Occur?

Zirconium alloys, or Zircaloys, are very popular in nuclear power plants as fuel rod casing. This is because they have a very low neutron capture cross-section, and are very unlikely to interfere with the neutrons emitted from the fuel within the rods. However metallic zirconium has a big disadvantage. At temperatures of above 1200°C, zirconium will react exothermically with water to oxidise, and produce free hydrogen gas.
  Zr + 2 H2O → ZrO2 + 2 H2 [8]
Not only does this reaction create hydrogen gas, but also it provides more heat to facilitate more reactions. The large amount of hydrogen gas produced is extremely combustible, forming explosive mixtures in air with as little as 4% concentration, that can be ignited by small amounts of heat, something that is definitely not is short supply in the middle of a meltdown.[11] The resulting explosion can severely damage containment buildings and cause large amounts of radioactive material to leak into the surrounding environment. This occured in reactors 1, 2, and 3 of the Fukushima disaster.[12]

What is a Criticality Accident?

In nuclear physics, a mass of nuclear material is said to be in a critical state when its fission chain reaction is self sustaining. This occurs when the conditions are right for the neutrons emitted by the reaction to cause another reaction in the same mass of material, allowing the fission reaction to continue inside the middle of the material. Although there are several factors that can cause criticality, the two most common factors are the size of the mass, and neutron reflection. Because neutrons need to travel a certain distance to lose energy before they can react, having a larger mass increases the possibility that a neutron will still be inside the mass of material when it is able to react.[13]
Neutron reflection will also cause criticality in fissile material. By using materials such as beryllium, you can reflect the neutrons emitted by the material back at the material. This means that almost every neutron produced will go on to create another reaction. A two famous examples of criticality accidents caused by neutron reflection are the deaths of Louis Slotin and Harry Daghlian. Although they were killed by two different experiments, both involved using beryllium to investigate criticality. Ironically the same radiation source was used in both experiments, a 6 kilogram sphere of plutonium aptly nicknamed the ‘Demon Core’, after causing the deaths of two people. The design of the core meant that it was always 95% critical so that it would be easy for scientists to achieve criticality for an experiment.[15] Louis Slotins experiment involved containing the core between two hemispheres of beryllium. The problem occured when Louis broke safety regulations and used the blade of a screwdriver to separate the hemispheres instead of the provided spacers. When the screwdriver fell out, the gap between the hemispheres was closed and the beryllium started to reflect all of the neutrons, causing the core to go critical.[14] When a nuclear mass becomes critical it emits neutron radiation, a form of ionising radiation as free neutrons. This radiation is extremely deadly to all forms of life. Louis was able to separate the hemispheres before the radiation became powerful enough to effect the 7 other people in the room, but not before he received a lethal dose of radiation himself.

Human Safety

What is a Dosimeter Badge?

A dosimeter badge, sometimes called a film badge, is a badge made for people working in close proximity to radiation. When providing medical care to someone who is suffering from radiation poisoning or a similar illness, knowing the intensity of the radiation dose they received is very helpful and allows you to make better decisions. A dosimeter badge works in a similar way to the film in a camera, in fact, the same type of film is used. Photographic film of varying grain size is placed into a plastic case behind several different metal filters. These metal filters are able to block certain types of radiation in some areas of the film, allowing for a more detailed reading of radiation exposure. The badge also contains a form of ID. This allows the badge to be used as a security badge while linking the readings from the badge to the person wearing it. When the film is read, it is removed from the plastic case to be developed. Because the film is photographic film it must be stored, even when sitting in the case, in a light protective packet. The film is developed in a dark room with similar techniques used to develop photographs, and the radiation is recorded based on the intensity of the ‘images’ that appear on each section of the film.[16]

How do Respiration Masks Work?

Although powerful rays of ionizing radiation are very harmful, it is more common to sustain damage from small airborne particles of radioactive material. Ionising alpha radiation can be emitted by tiny flakes of radioactive material.[17] Alpha particles (when produced by radioactive decay) have a low penetration strength, and are able to be stopped by a few centimeters of air or just skin.[18] But if the tiny particles of radioactive material were they to be inhaled, they could settle in the lungs and emit radiation right next to the body's most important and delicate organs. Because the radioactive particles released by the material have low penetration strengths, respiration equipment does not need to shield against radiation using metals like lead. Instead they just need to filter out dust and small particles, making them no different from the respiration equipment used in other industries. Ironically one of the most important pieces of radiation protection has very little to do with radiation in particular.

How are Radiation suits Designed?

Most forms of radiation protection are designed off the fact that radioactive particles are able to damage human cells due to their large amount of energy. If the energy of these particles can be removed, then they will pose little to no threat to the body. This is simple enough to do with weak forms of radiation such as x-rays. Lead aprons are used around x-ray machines to protect vulnerable parts of the body, or to protect doctors who have longer exposure.[19] As the x-rays pass through the lead, the high density and electron count of the metal dissipate the energy of the x-rays and stop them dealing damage.[20] A relatively thin layer of lead is enough to block x-rays, but it is much harder to protect against the higher energy gamma rays. The best gamma protection available is the ‘StemRad 360 Gamma’. The 360 Gamma is a cross between a vest and a belt, designed to protect the body from radiation poisoning.[21] While the shield covers a small amount of the body compared to a full apron, it is designed to fully protect the pelvis bone. This is because humans suffer the most damage when radiation effects the bone marrow and stops the production of blood cells. By protecting the pelvis, a large amount of marrow can be protected, allowing the body to naturally regenerate the rest of the bone marrow.[22]

Human Effect

What is Radiation Poisoning?

Acute Radiation Syndrome, commonly called radiation poisoning, is the name given to a group of several negative health effects that are caused by exposure to a high dose of ionising radiation.[24] Depending on the dose you receive, the results can be extremely severe and onset very quickly. For example, patients receiving 2-6 grays on radiation can experience nausea and vomiting within 2 hours, while patients receiving 30+ grays will experience severe headaches and seizures within 1 hours. There are three main areas that are affected; the blood system, the nervous system, and the gastrointestinal system. The blood system is damaged through aplastic anemia, which is when the bone marrow is damaged leading to a drop in the amount of blood cells.[25] This is not fatal in small doses, but can complicate other infuries like burns or cuts, because there aren’t enough white blood cells to heal the body. Aplastic anemia occurs when the radiation damages the bone marrow. The bone marrow is particularly vulnerable because it is all over the body and close under the skin, making it an easy target for radiation with low penetration strength. Gastrointestinal symptoms are common because the body's digestive system involves many small parts. These are particularly vulnerable to radiation because a smaller amount of cells being damaged has a larger effect.
By far the most fatal symptoms are neurovascular. Due to how delicate the brain is, and how wide spread the nervous system is, when receiving high doses of radiation neurovascular damage is common. Neurovascular damage almost always leads to death after seizures, ataxia and loss of consciousness. When ionising radiation is able to kill important cells in the brain, these symptoms are very easy to develop and highly damaging.

What are Radiation Burns?

Radiation burns are areas of the skin that have been damaged by radiation. ‘Burn’ is used in its wider sense as ‘damage to the skin’ like with chemical or cold burns, as opposed to the more common sense of heat injury.[26] Radiation burns are actually extremely common, with millions of people each year getting sunburns. Sunburns are a type of radiation burn as it is not heat, but the direct UV radiation that damages the skin. Sunburn occurs when radiation from the sun penetrates the skin and damages the DNA in the skin cells.[27] This causes the cells to die from apoptosis (cell suicide) but not before increasing the blood flow to the area, causing redness and a hot sensation. This hot sensation is commonly misinterpreted as the skin still burning, furthering the misconception that heat, not radiation, causes sunburn.
Radiation burns from stronger forms of radiation work the same way, just on a larger scale. Gamma rays can penetrate much deeper into the skin, as they have more energy, causing what some describe as a “three dimensional sunburn”.[28]

How can you get Cancer from Radiation?

While most interactions between cells and radiation damage or destroy the cell, sometimes the cell, or rather the cells DNA, can become mutated.[29] This is when the cell is affected enough to change how it works, but can still survive. This is often worse as it leads to cancer forming when the affected cell reproduces at an uncontrolled rate. Radiation is said to be responsible for 10% of invasive cancers.
Ionising radiation causes atoms to lose their electrons and become ions. This breaks chemical bonds and changes the structure of the molecule. If the cell is changed in the right way, the cell can become cancerous.
